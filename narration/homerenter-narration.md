# HomeRenter — Audio Narration Script

---

Hey, thanks for listening. This is the HomeRenter case study — a smaller, earlier piece of work that I keep in my portfolio because it taught me something I still use today: don't optimise for the wrong problem.

---

## What is HomeRenter?

HomeRenter set out to be a fairer, low-cost alternative to traditional letting agents — connecting landlords, renters, keyholders and tradespeople in one place. Founded by landlords and property developers, the site looked great but hid some tech and conversion issues.

To drive growth, the company introduced a 30-day free listing period alongside its low-cost subscription, giving landlords access to Rightmove and Zoopla to advertise their properties. Paid add-ons like gas safety certificates rounded out the offering.

It competed with online platforms like OpenRent, HouseSimple, Urban, Upad, and Howsy — as well as traditional high-street estate agents.

---

## My Role

I joined as the sole UX/UI designer, working closely with the product manager and lead developer. Initially tasked with shaping the online rental service vision, I also investigated why the platform wasn't converting as many customers as expected.

---

## So What Was the Problem?

Product decisions focused on landlords because they were the paying users with the most monetisation potential. But despite the free offer, the company wasn't attracting as many landlords as expected.

When I joined, a new property-creation flow was being implemented to reduce steps — this was considered the faster, preferred experience. But I spent a few weeks on customer support: auditing emails, speaking with landlords, and weighing business value against their actual issues.

The findings surprised me. A good percentage of landlords tolerated property creation despite a few hiccups. They were more frustrated by the confusing post-offer tenant experience — some were even paying competitors £100 to £200 just for a simpler handover. The free offer and subscription also felt complicated and unclear, causing people to pause and call us.

Compared to competitors, the site was attracting more live-in landlords looking for lodgers — unlikely to buy extras like photography or key-holder services — and professional landlords who were exploiting the free listing by cycling through email addresses while handling everything else themselves.

Both were poor fits for the business model.

---

## Recommendations and Quick Fixes

Based on the research, I recommended targeted design and copy updates to fix the core mismatch and increase traffic from the company's advertising.

First: landlords weren't discovering services like referencing and photography because they were buried in the dashboard. Adding a checkout-style step within property creation surfaced these add-ons at the right moment, while keeping them optional.

Second: landlords were forced to write full descriptions before moving on. A simple reorder would let them start with shorter quick-description tags like parking or ensuite.

Third: offer a clear one-off payment option, because the subscription created hesitation for landlords who only needed to let one property.

Fourth: unexpected charges were a recurring theme in support emails — making free trial terms explicit, like "No credit card needed," would reduce sign-up anxiety.

Fifth: user testing revealed landlords were unclear about what HomeRenter even offered. I simplified the landing page, restructured the FAQs, and removed distractions like tradesmen and keyholders.

Sixth: the site asked for trust it hadn't earned yet. No let properties on display, no testimonials. Showcasing recently let properties — even if manually updated every few weeks — was a low-cost way to build credibility quickly.

Seventh, and the toughest discussion: removing "One Stop Shop" from multiple landing pages. The tagline tested well, but it overstated the breadth and geographic reach of the service, blurring who the platform was actually built for. That gap between promise and reality was causing landlords to choose competitors.

---

## The Outcome

Due to the founder taking extended leave, broader strategic decisions were paused and some recommendations couldn't move forward. Despite these constraints, progress was made where possible.

Some recommendations were implemented within the first month, including copy changes that increased site traffic. I observed an estimated 50% rise in properties approved for Rightmove advertising, though conversion to paid add-ons remained low.

This was an early and important lesson: making something faster doesn't matter if you're optimising the wrong journey. Early investigation — before building — is now a habit I bring to every project.
